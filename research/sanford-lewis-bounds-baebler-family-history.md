# Scope and Method

This document records the verifiable military service and ancestry history of fourteen individuals across four family lines — Bounds (maternal blood), Lewis (paternal blood), Sanford (married in), and Baebler / Clark / Cantrell (maternal extended through Judy Baebler). It is compiled for Tricia Sanford from facts she provided, web research conducted May 26, 2026, and supporting historical context.

**Three rules govern this document:**

1. **Verified facts** are statements supported by a publicly-accessible web source. Each one carries an inline citation with a URL. If no source could be found, the document says so explicitly.
2. **Family memory** is recorded in its own clearly-labeled subsection for each person. It is not promoted to "verified" unless an external source corroborates it.
3. **Theories** are labeled as theories, with a note of what would confirm or refute each one.

**Records the document does *not* claim to have accessed:** the National Personnel Records Center (NPRC) Official Military Personnel Files; the VA Claims Files (C-files); the FBI's individual file holdings beyond what is in the public FBI Vault; sealed Missouri penitentiary and Department of Corrections records; and any paywalled genealogy database (Ancestry image viewer, Fold3, Newspapers.com behind paywall). Every record of those types appears only in the **Next Steps** sections, with the form, URL, and office to request it.

**The 1973 NPRC fire** destroyed approximately 80% of US Army personnel files for service members discharged between November 1, 1912 and January 1, 1960, and approximately 75% of US Air Force files for members discharged between September 25, 1947 and January 1, 1964 with surnames alphabetically between "Hubbard" and "Z". Navy and Marine Corps records were stored separately and generally survived. This is flagged on every entry where it applies. [Source: National Archives — *The 1973 Fire* — https://www.archives.gov/personnel-records-center/fire-1973]

### Verified facts and external corroboration (web research)

- **The 4th Strategic Aerospace Division (also known as the 4th Air Division)** was a SAC intermediate command echelon. Reactivated in 1951 (Barksdale AFB, Louisiana); moved to Grand Forks AFB, North Dakota in 1964; **moved to Francis E. Warren AFB, Wyoming in July 1971; inactivated August 23, 1988.** At its 1971+ F.E. Warren station the division controlled four Minuteman ICBM wings and two B-52 bomber wings. The "Strategic Aerospace Division" designation came from a spring-1962 SAC-wide renaming for divisions that controlled both bombers and missiles. [Source: USAF biography of BG Gerald G. Fall Jr. (commanded 4th SAD) — https://www.af.mil/About-Us/Biographies/Display/Article/107114/brigadier-general-gerald-g-fall-jr/; Source: Wikipedia — 4th Air Division — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4th_Air_Division; Source: F.E. Warren AFB History — https://www.warren.af.mil/About-Us/Base-History/]
- **Therefore James Bounds's final duty station was almost certainly F.E. Warren AFB, Wyoming**, given his October 1974 release falls within the 1971–1988 F.E. Warren window. (To be confirmed against his DD-214.)
- **Service number prefix analysis.** His service number (held in the family file, not reproduced here) carries the "AF" prefix identifying Air Force. The leading two-digit geographic code on his number matches the **WWII Army Selective Service code for the Eighth Corps Area**, which covered **Texas, Oklahoma, Colorado, New Mexico, and Arizona** (headquartered Fort Sam Houston, Texas). Post-1948 Regular Air Force enlistments used the 11-million through 19-million number range; post-1948 AF draftees used the 50–59 million range — **neither matches his number's prefix.** The most consistent interpretation is that James Bounds was **originally a WWII-era Army or Army Air Forces Selective Service draftee** whose number was **retained with the new "AF" prefix when the US Air Force became a separate service in 1947**. This is consistent with the family record of WWII service. [Source: Wikipedia — Service number (USAF) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_number_(United_States_Air_Force); Source: Wikipedia — Service number (US Army) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_number_(United_States_Army); Source: Amy Johnson Crow — How to Decode a WWII US Army Serial Number — https://www.amyjohnsoncrow.com/how-to-decode-a-wwii-army-serial-number/]
- **B-29 Superfortress** — WWII-era four-engine piston heavy bomber; equipped SAC at its 1946 formation; phased out of SAC by end of 1954. [Source: National Museum of the USAF / SAC — https://www.nationalmuseum.af.mil/Visit/Museum-Exhibits/Fact-Sheets/Display/Article/197645/strategic-air-command/]
- **B-47 Stratojet** — SAC's first jet strategic bomber, swept-wing medium; operational in SAC from 1953, retired from the bombardment role by 1965. [Source: Wikipedia — B-47 Stratojet — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_B-47_Stratojet]
- **B-52 Stratofortress** — heavy jet bomber; entered SAC service April 1955; still in service today. [Source: Wikipedia — B-52 Stratofortress — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress; Source: Air & Space Forces Magazine — Fifty Years of the B-52 — https://www.airandspaceforces.com/article/1201buff/]
- The B-29 → B-47 → B-52 progression matches the documented SAC bomber mechanic career arc spanning the late 1940s through the 1970s.

### Theories / open questions

- **Theory:** James Bounds was drafted in the WWII Eighth Corps Area (TX/OK/CO/NM/AZ), most likely Texas given Fort Sam Houston was the corps HQ. Family knowledge of where he was living when drafted would confirm.
- **Likely final station:** F.E. Warren AFB, Cheyenne, Wyoming.

## 5. Ronald "Ron" Bounds — Uncle

> Per Tricia's instruction, this entry records service facts only. No character commentary. No romanticizing.
>
> **Verification status:** the service narrative below comes from family memory. The NPRC Official Military Personnel File has not yet been pulled. Treat unit assignment, discharge characterization, and the upgrade pathway as **pending NPRC verification** until the DD-214 and discharge-review decisional document are in hand.

### Family memory and family-documented facts (per Tricia Sanford)

- Date of birth: **November 26, 1950.**
- US Army, 101st Airborne Division.
- Survived the Battle of Hamburger Hill (Hill 937 / Dong Ap Bia, A Shau Valley, Vietnam, May 10–20, 1969). Age 18 at the time.
- After the battle, convinced fellow Hamburger Hill survivors to desert with him. Went to Sweden.
- Initially received a Dishonorable Discharge.
- His father, USAF MSgt James Bounds (Person 3), used Air Force connections to obtain an upgrade to Other Than Honorable via Discharge Review Board.
- Returned to the United States.
- Heroin addiction. In and out of prison. Deceased.

### Verified facts and external corroboration (web research)

- **All three candidate battalions of the 101st Airborne Division named in family research — 3rd Battalion 187th Infantry "Rakkasans"; 1st Battalion 506th Infantry "Currahees"; and 2nd Battalion 501st Infantry — are independently confirmed as the assault battalions on Hill 937, May 10–20, 1969, during Operation Apache Snow.** All three were in the 3rd Brigade, 101st Airborne Division (Airmobile). The 3/187 under Lt. Col. Weldon "Tiger" Honeycutt bore the heaviest fighting and casualties on the hill itself, conducting the main north-face assaults. The 1/506 attacked from the south and joined the two-battalion final assault on May 18–20. The 2/501 was committed later in the battle as reinforcement. US casualties reported as 72 KIA / 372 WIA. [Source: Wikipedia — Battle of Hamburger Hill — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Hamburger_Hill; Source: army.mil — Rakkasans on Hamburger Hill — https://www.army.mil/article/38997/rakkasans_on_hamburger_hill; Source: Warfare History Network — https://warfarehistorynetwork.com/article/the-battle-of-hamurger-hill-101st-airborne-hill-937/; Source: army.mil — Vietnam 40 years later — https://www.army.mil/article/79744/]
- **Senator Edward Kennedy denounced the operation on the Senate floor May 20, 1969** as "senseless and irresponsible … American boys are too valuable to be sacrificed for a false sense of military pride." [Source: Edward M. Kennedy archives — http://www.tedkennedy.org/ownwords/event/vietnam.html; Source: History.com — Hamburger Hill begins — https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/may-11/paratroopers-battle-for-hamburger-hill]
- **The June 27, 1969 issue of LIFE magazine — "The Faces of the American Dead in Vietnam: One Week's Toll" — printed 242 portraits of men killed May 28 – June 3, 1969.** Only about five of those portraits were Hamburger Hill dead, but public conflation of the photo essay with the battle accelerated the political turn against attritional ground assaults. [Source: TIME — Faces of the American Dead in Vietnam — https://time.com/3485726/faces-of-the-american-dead-in-vietnam-one-weeks-toll-june-1969/; Source: LIFE.com retrospective — https://www.life.com/history/faces-of-the-american-dead-in-vietnam-one-weeks-toll-june-1969/]
- **Sweden was the only nation to grant formal asylum status to US military deserters during the Vietnam War.** Roughly 800–1,000 American resisters reached Sweden between April 1967 and March 1973; about two-thirds were military deserters (not draft evaders). Sweden granted humanitarian asylum (not refugee status); the policy caused the United States to recall its ambassador to Stockholm in 1969. [Source: Wikipedia — Vietnam War resisters in Sweden — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War_resisters_in_Sweden; Source: Literary Hub — The Vietnam War Deserters Who Sought Asylum in Sweden — https://lithub.com/the-vietnam-war-deserters-who-sought-asylum-in-sweden/]
- **Carter's Proclamation 4483 (January 21, 1977) pardoned draft evaders only.** It did **not** cover Uniform Code of Military Justice offenses including desertion. Deserters were addressed earlier under **Ford's Presidential Clemency Program (Executive Order 11803 / Proclamation 4313, September 16, 1974)**, which required up to 24 months of alternate service and produced a "clemency discharge" that did not restore VA benefits. After the Ford program closed, individual case-by-case discharge review remained the path forward — which is consistent with the family-recorded upgrade pathway. [Source: Wikipedia — Proclamation 4483 — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_4483; Source: National Archives codified text — https://www.archives.gov/federal-register/codification/proclamations/04483.html; Source: Miller Center — Ford clemency remarks — https://millercenter.org/the-presidency/presidential-speeches/september-16-1974-remarks-clemency-vietnam-era-draft-evaders; Source: GAO FPCD-76-64 — The Clemency Program of 1974 — https://www.gao.gov/assets/fpcd-76-64.pdf]
- **Army Discharge Review Board (ADRB) jurisdictional note.** The ADRB reviews discharges issued within the previous 15 years; older cases go to the Army Board for Correction of Military Records (ABCMR). The ADRB cannot upgrade a court-martial Dishonorable Discharge — those can only be modified by the ABCMR. **This means the family-stated upgrade from "Dishonorable" to "Other Than Honorable" most likely went through one of two paths:** (a) the original characterization was actually a Bad Conduct Discharge or an administrative Other Than Honorable (rather than a court-martial DD), and ADRB upgraded it; or (b) the action went through ABCMR. [Source: Army Review Boards Agency — https://www.army.mil/arba; Source: Army Discharge Review Board FAQ — https://arba.army.pentagon.mil/adrb-faq.html; Source: Swords to Plowshares — Upgrading Your Discharge — https://www.swords-to-plowshares.org/guides/upgrading-your-discharge]
- **Lee Robins's 1974 study *The Vietnam Drug User Returns*** (Special Action Office for Drug Abuse Prevention Monograph A-2) followed 965 returning servicemen and found roughly 34% had used heroin in Vietnam and roughly 20% met dependence criteria, yet only about 1% were re-addicted in the first year home and 5–12% relapsed over 1–3 years. The study upended the prevailing belief that opioid addiction was effectively permanent — though a meaningful minority did relapse, and Vietnam-era addiction is well documented as a vector into civilian opioid use, criminal-justice involvement, and downstream mortality. [Source: ERIC ED134912 — https://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED134912; Source: Robins, *AJPH* 64 Supp. (Dec 1974) — https://ajph.aphapublications.org/doi/pdf/10.2105/AJPH.64.12_Suppl.38; Source: Hall & Weier, *Addiction* (2017) — https://kclpure.kcl.ac.uk/ws/portalfiles/portal/61077174/Addiction_classics_Robins_preprint.pdf]
- **Family-cited book correction.** The family file references "Hans Lagerberg, *American Deserters in Sweden*." This title could not be verified. The closest verifiable English-language work is **Thomas Lee Hayes, *American Deserters in Sweden: The Men and Their Challenge*, Association Press, 1971.** Worth confirming whether the family meant Hayes, or a separate Swedish-language Lagerberg work. [Source: Hayes — Amazon listing — https://www.amazon.com/American-deserters-Sweden-their-challenge/dp/0809618176]

Between July 1969 and October 1970 the battalion's Rome Plows cleared roughly **240,000 acres** of jungle in South Vietnam in support of II Field Force. The battalion served in 14 Vietnam campaigns. [Source: Veterans Breakfast Club — *How to Clear a Jungle: The 62d Engineer Battalion (Land Clearing) in Vietnam* — https://veteransbreakfastclub.org/how-to-clear-a-jungle-the-62d-engineer-battalion-land-clearing-in-vietnam/; Source: GlobalSecurity — 62nd Engineer Combat Battalion — https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/agency/army/62eng.htm]

So **a Rome Plow operator who appears on the rolls of both the 62nd Engineer Battalion and the 984th LC Company is structurally normal: the 984th was a subordinate company of the 62nd.** Targets included the Iron Triangle, Ho Bo Woods, and Bo Loi Woods. [Source: Vietnam Landclearers Association — https://vietnamlandclearers.org/; Source: Vietnam Landclearers — Rome Plow Companies — https://vietnamlandclearers.org/media/rome-plow-companies.html; Source: US Army CMH — *U.S. Army Engineers, 1965–1970* (Ploger), Ch. 7 — http://webdoc.sub.gwdg.de/ebook/p/2005/CMH_2/www.army.mil/cmh-pg/books/vietnam/engineers/ch7.htm]

[Source: Vietnam Unit Files — 20th Engineer Brigade — https://vietnamunitfiles.com/20th-engineer-brigade/]

# Historical Context Appendix

## Pearl Harbor — December 7, 1941

The Imperial Japanese Navy launched a surprise carrier-based air attack on the US Pacific Fleet at Pearl Harbor, Oahu. Two waves totaling roughly 350 aircraft from six carriers, beginning at approximately 7:55 a.m. Hawaii time, sank or damaged 19 US ships (including four battleships sunk) and killed 2,403 Americans. The United States declared war on Japan the next day. [Source: Pearl Harbor National Memorial / NPS — https://www.nps.gov/articles/pearlattackww2.htm; Source: Naval History and Heritage Command — Pearl Harbor — https://www.history.navy.mil/research/library/online-reading-room/title-list-alphabetically/p/pearl-harbor-survivor-reports.html]

## Battle of the Bulge — December 16, 1944 – January 25, 1945

The last major German offensive on the Western Front. A 75-mile-front surprise attack through the Ardennes Forest of Belgium and Luxembourg aimed at splitting Allied lines and seizing the port of Antwerp. Over 200,000 German troops and ~1,000 tanks initially overran thin American positions, creating the "bulge" in the line, but were halted at strongpoints including Bastogne (101st Airborne) and St. Vith. Over 600,000 US troops eventually fought in it — the largest battle in US Army history — with US casualties of roughly 75,000–80,000 (including 19,246 killed per the official Army count). [Source: US Army Center of Military History — https://www.history.army.mil/html/reference/bulge/; Source: National WWII Museum — https://www.nationalww2museum.org/war/articles/battle-of-the-bulge; Source: US Army — https://www.army.mil/botb/]

## 1973 NPRC Fire — July 12, 1973

A fire reported shortly after midnight on July 12, 1973 at the National Personnel Records Center building at 9700 Page Boulevard in St. Louis, Missouri burned out of control for 22 hours and was not declared fully out until July 16. It destroyed an estimated **16–18 million** US military personnel records — approximately **80% of Army personnel records for discharges November 1, 1912 – January 1, 1960**, and approximately **75% of Air Force records for discharges September 25, 1947 – January 1, 1964** (surnames Hubbard through Z). **No backup copies or microfilms existed.** Navy and Marine Corps records were stored separately and generally survived. NPRC offers a reconstruction process using auxiliary records (Final Pay Vouchers, Morning Reports, VA index). [Source: NARA — *The 1973 Fire* — https://www.archives.gov/personnel-records-center/fire-1973; Source: NARA Fact Sheet — https://www.archives.gov/press/press-kits/2023-nprc-fire-anniversary/nprc-fire-anniversary-factsheet]

## Battle of Hamburger Hill — May 10–20, 1969

Officially the **Battle of Dong Ap Bia**; "Hamburger Hill" was the soldier's name for Hill 937 in the A Shau Valley of Vietnam. Part of Operation Apache Snow. ## Sweden as Asylum for US Deserters During the Vietnam War

Sweden was the only nation to grant formal asylum status to US military deserters during the Vietnam War. Roughly 800–1,000 American resisters reached Sweden between April 1967 and March 1973; about two-thirds were military deserters (not draft evaders). Sweden granted humanitarian asylum (not refugee status); the policy caused the United States to recall its ambassador to Stockholm in 1969. Most resisters returned to the US or moved on to Canada within a decade; fewer than 100 remained as permanent émigrés. **Note on legal coda: Carter's Proclamation 4483 (1977) pardoned draft evaders only; deserters were handled separately under Ford's 1974 Clemency Program (Proclamation 4313), which required up to 24 months of alternate service for a "clemency discharge" that did not restore VA benefits.** After both programs closed, individual case-by-case discharge review (ADRB or ABCMR) remained the path. The most reliable English-language reference on the Swedish deserter community is **Thomas Lee Hayes, *American Deserters in Sweden: The Men and Their Challenge*, Association Press, 1971** — *not* a Hans Lagerberg book of that title, which could not be verified. [Source: Wikipedia — Vietnam War resisters in Sweden — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War_resisters_in_Sweden; Source: Literary Hub — https://lithub.com/the-vietnam-war-deserters-who-sought-asylum-in-sweden/; Source: Wikipedia — Proclamation 4483 — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclamation_4483; Source: Miller Center — Ford clemency remarks — https://millercenter.org/the-presidency/presidential-speeches/september-16-1974-remarks-clemency-vietnam-era-draft-evaders; Source: GAO — *The Clemency Program of 1974* — https://www.gao.gov/assets/fpcd-76-64.pdf]

## Vietnam Heroin Epidemic and Operation Golden Flow

High-purity (~90–96%) Southeast Asian heroin became cheaply available to US troops in Vietnam beginning in spring 1970. Surveys put in-country heroin use among enlisted men around 34–43%, with about half of users meeting dependence criteria. In May 1971 President Nixon declared drug abuse "public enemy number one" and stood up the Special Action Office for Drug Abuse Prevention (SAODAP) under Dr. Jerome Jaffe. **Operation Golden Flow** (September 1971) required a clean urinalysis before any service member could board their "freedom bird" home. Dr. Lee Robins's 1974 study *The Vietnam Drug User Returns* (SAODAP Monograph A-2) followed 965 returning servicemen and found ~34% had used heroin in Vietnam and ~20% met dependence criteria, yet only about 1% were re-addicted in the first year home and 5–12% relapsed over 1–3 years — upending the prevailing belief that opioid addiction was effectively permanent. A meaningful minority did relapse, and Vietnam-era addiction is well documented as a vector into civilian opioid use, criminal-justice involvement, and downstream mortality. [Source: Wikipedia — Operation Golden Flow — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Golden_Flow; Source: History.com — GIs' Drug Use in Vietnam Soared — https://www.history.com/articles/drug-use-in-vietnam; Source: VA History — *Borne in Battle* — https://department.va.gov/history/featured-stories/borne-in-battle-va-treatment-for-addiction-after-vietnam/; Source: ERIC ED134912 — Robins, *The Vietnam Drug User Returns* — https://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED134912; Source: ScienceDirect — *Twenty-five year mortality of US servicemen deployed in Vietnam*]

## US Army Discharge Upgrade Process

The **Army Discharge Review Board (ADRB)**, under the Army Review Boards Agency at Fort Belvoir, reviews discharges issued within the previous 15 years on **DD Form 293**, submitted via ACTSOnline. After 15 years, the **Army Board for Correction of Military Records (ABCMR)** has jurisdiction; applications use **DD Form 149**. The petitioner must demonstrate the discharge was **improper** (issued in error or in violation of law/regulation) or **inequitable** (inconsistent with policies/traditions). A **Dishonorable Discharge issued by a general court-martial** is generally **outside** ADRB jurisdiction and can only be modified by the ABCMR. The 2014 Hagel memo and 2017 Kurta memo liberalized standards for PTSD/TBI/mental-health-related upgrades, but those post-date a 1970s-era upgrade. [Source: Army Review Boards Agency — https://www.army.mil/arba; Source: ADRB FAQ — https://arba.army.pentagon.mil/adrb-faq.html; Source: National Archives — Correcting Military Service Records — https://www.archives.gov/veterans/military-service-records/correct-service-records.html; Source: Swords to Plowshares — Upgrading Your Discharge — https://www.swords-to-plowshares.org/guides/upgrading-your-discharge]

## Rome Plows and Agent Orange

From 1962–1971 US forces sprayed approximately **19 million gallons of herbicide** in Vietnam under Operation Ranch Hand, including roughly **11.2 million gallons of Agent Orange**. Once foliage was killed, ground forces moved in to physically remove the woody debris and tree stumps — the dominant tool was the **Rome Plow**: a Caterpillar D7E (and some D9) bulldozer fitted with a two-ton "stinger" cutting blade manufactured by the **Rome Plow Company of Rome, Georgia**, plus a heavy protective cage. By December 1968 the Army had organized dedicated **Land Clearing Companies of 30 plows each, six total — three assigned to the 20th Engineer Brigade and three to the 18th Engineer Brigade**, operating under the **62nd Engineer Battalion (Land Clearing)** and counterparts. They cleared **100–200 acres of triple-canopy jungle per day**, often through terrain that had already been chemically defoliated, putting operators in sustained direct contact with herbicide-saturated soil, splintered biomass, and burning slash. VA today extends a **blanket presumption of herbicide exposure** to anyone who served in Vietnam between **January 9, 1962 and May 7, 1975**, with ~23 presumptive conditions for service connection (expanded under the PACT Act of 2022). [Source: NCBI / IOM — *Veterans and Agent Orange* — https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK236347/; Source: Wikipedia — Rome plow — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome_plow; Source: Veterans Breakfast Club — 62nd Engineer Battalion Land Clearing — https://veteransbreakfastclub.org/how-to-clear-a-jungle-the-62d-engineer-battalion-land-clearing-in-vietnam/; Source: US Army CMH — *U.S. Army Engineers, 1965–1970* (Ploger) Ch. 7 — http://webdoc.sub.gwdg.de/ebook/p/2005/CMH_2/www.army.mil/cmh-pg/books/vietnam/engineers/ch7.htm; Source: VA — Agent Orange — https://www.va.gov/disability/eligibility/hazardous-materials-exposure/agent-orange/]

## 89th Airlift Wing and the Air Force One Mission

The 89th Airlift Wing, based at Joint Base Andrews, Maryland, is the Air Force's Special Air Mission organization providing global airlift, logistics, and secure communications for the President, Vice President, Cabinet, members of Congress, and senior military leaders. Its Presidential Airlift Group operates the two VC-25A aircraft — heavily modified Boeing 747-200Bs that carry the "Air Force One" call sign whenever the President is aboard — which entered service in 1990. The wing traces its lineage to a 1949 Air Force Reserve troop-carrier wing, was redesignated the 89th Military Airlift Wing in 1966, and became the 89th Airlift Wing in 1991. Its fleet today also includes C-32A (Boeing 757) and C-37A/B (Gulfstream) executive transports. ## Operation Southern Watch

Operation Southern Watch was the US-led coalition air operation enforcing the no-fly zone over southern Iraq, established **August 27, 1992** under UN Security Council Resolution 688 (April 5, 1991), which condemned Saddam Hussein's repression of the Iraqi population including the Marsh Arabs and Shia in southern Iraq. The zone initially prohibited Iraqi fixed- and rotary-wing flight **below the 32nd parallel**; in 1996 the boundary was extended north to the **33rd parallel**. Conducted by Joint Task Force Southwest Asia under US Central Command, with the Air Force component (US Central Command Air Forces / Ninth Air Force) **headquartered at Shaw AFB, South Carolina**. Coalition partners included the UK, France (until late 1996), and Saudi Arabia, with logistical support from 22 other nations. OSW ran continuously for more than ten years until it was folded into Operation Iraqi Freedom on **April 14, 2003**. ## White House Greetings Office / Presidential Birthday Recognition

The White House Greetings Office — a tradition of nearly 70 years — sends signed congratulatory cards from the President and First Lady for milestone occasions on free public request. Birthdays qualify starting at **age 80 for civilians and age 70 for veterans**; anniversaries at 50 years; **centenarians (100+) are explicitly covered**. Requests must be submitted **at least six weeks before the date**, online at https://www.whitehouse.gov/greetings/ or through a constituent's US Senator or Representative office. The card is mailed in a White House envelope with a printed presidential signature. **The White House does not routinely retain copies of greetings it sends — the physical card goes to the recipient and is the only artifact.** [Source: White House Greetings Office — https://www.whitehouse.gov/greetings/]

## Eastern European Roma / Porajmos

The **Porajmos** (Romani: *"the Devouring"*) refers to the Nazi genocide of Europe's Roma and Sinti people during World War II. Per the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum, historians estimate that the Germans and their Axis allies killed **at least 250,000 European Roma during WWII**, with some scholars estimating the full death toll may reach **500,000** — roughly one-quarter of Europe's pre-war Romani population. Romani people were shot in mass executions across occupied eastern Poland, the Soviet Union, and Serbia, and were also murdered in killing centers including Auschwitz-Birkenau, where of approximately 23,000 Roma and Sinti inmates, about 21,000 died or were killed. Persecution began in 1933 with discriminatory measures, internment in *Zigeunerlager* ("gypsy camps"), and forced sterilization under Nazi racial laws. [Source: USHMM Holocaust Encyclopedia — *Genocide of European Roma (Gypsies), 1939–1945* — https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/article/genocide-of-european-roma-gypsies-1939-1945]
